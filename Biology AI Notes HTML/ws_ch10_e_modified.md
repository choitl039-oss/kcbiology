# 10 Transpiration, transport and support in plants

## 10.1 Transpiration (Book 1B, p. 10-2)

- (1) Transpiration (蒸騰) is the loss of water in the form of water vapour from plant surfaces due to evaporation.
- Water is mainly lost through (2) stomata, (3) cuticle and (4) lenticels.

### B. Process of transpiration (Book 1B, p. 10-4)

1. Water diffuses out of the mesophyll cells to form a (5) water film on the cell surface.
2. Water of the water film (6) evaporates to form water vapour. The water vapour moves into the air space among the mesophyll cells.
3. Water vapour in the air space (7) diffuses through the stomata to the atmosphere.

### Creation of transpiration pull

- During transpiration, mesophyll cells near the air space lose water to the air space. Their water potential (8) lowers (lowers / rises).
- Water moves from neighbouring cells into these cells by (9) osmosis. This is repeated across the layer of mesophyll cells.
- Eventually, water moves out of the (10) xylem vessels by osmosis to replace the water loss in mesophyll cells. Transpiration pull (蒸騰拉力) is created to pull water up the xylem vessels from roots.

### C. Significance of transpiration (Book 1B, p. 10-5)

1. Transpiration creates transpiration pull, which enables (11) water and (12) minerals to be transported from the roots to other parts of the plant.
2. Transpiration pull facilitates the (13) absorption of water by roots.
3. During transpiration, the evaporation of water from the mesophyll cells removes (14) heat from the leaves. This produces a cooling effect to prevent plants from being (15) overheated under hot conditions.

### D. Adaptations of leaves to prevent excessive water loss (Book 1B, p. 10-6)

| Feature of leaves of terrestrial plants | Adaptation |
| --- | --- |
| Epidermis covered with a waxy (16) cuticle | Cuticle is almost impermeable to water. This reduces water loss through leaf surfaces. |
| Few or no stomata on the (17) upper (upper / lower) epidermis of most dicotyledonous leaves | Reduces water loss from the leaf surface that receives more direct sunlight. |
| (18) Guard cells are present to control the opening and closing of stomata | At night, the need for gas exchange in plants decreases. Most stomata (19) close (close / open) to reduce water loss from the leaves. |

### E. Distribution of stomata on leaves of different types of plants (Book 1B, p. 10-8)

| --- | --- |

| Terrestrial monocot plants | The stomata are almost (21) equally distributed on both sides of the leaves. As the leaves are oriented vertically, both sides of the leaves receive similar sunlight. They lose water at a (22) similar rate. |

### G. Environmental factors affecting the rate of transpiration (Book 1B, p. 10-13)

- Light intensity, (29) temperature, air movement and relative humidity are some of the environmental factors that affect the rate of transpiration of a plant.

#### Light intensity

- The rate of transpiration is low in darkness.

  Reason: The stomata are (30) closed. Only a small amount of water vapour diffuses out of the leaves.

- The rate (31) increases when the light intensity increases.

  Reason: As light intensity increases, the stomata open (32) wider. The cross-sectional area for the diffusion of water vapour (33) increases. Water vapour diffuses out of the leaves more rapidly.

#### Temperature

- The rate increases when the temperature (34) increases.

  Reason: As temperature increases, the rate of (35) evaporation of water from the surfaces of mesophyll cells increases. This increases the concentration gradient between the air space and the surrounding air. As a result, water vapour diffuses out of the leaves more rapidly.

#### Air movement

- The rate of transpiration is low in still air.

  Reason: Water vapour (36) accumulates around the stomata. This decreases the concentration gradient of water vapour between the air space and the surrounding air. The rate of diffusion of water out of the leaves decreases.

- The rate increases when the wind speed increases.

  Reason: Wind blows away water vapour around the stomata. This helps maintain a steep (37) concentration gradient of water vapour between the air space and the surrounding air. Water vapour diffuses out of the leaves more rapidly.

- The rate (38) decreases when the wind is too strong.

  Reason: Under strong wind, water may be lost from the plant too rapidly, causing most stomata to (39) close. Less water vapour diffuses out of the leaves.

#### Relative humidity

- The rate (40) decreases when the relative humidity of the surrounding air increases.

  Reason: A higher relative humidity of the surrounding air (41) decreases the concentration gradient of water vapour between the air space and the surrounding air. Less water vapour diffuses out of the leaves.

- Besides environmental factors, the structure of the plant (e.g. surface area of leaves, (42) thickness of the cuticle and abundance of (43) stomata) also affects the rate of transpiration.

## 10.2 Absorption of water and minerals by roots (Book 1B, p. 10-18)

### A. Structure of the root (Book 1B, p. 10-18)

#### Diagram labels

- (1) epidermis (表皮)
- (2) cortex (皮層)
- (3) xylem
- (4) phloem
- (5) root cap (根冠)

| Structure | Feature and function |
| --- | --- |
| Epidermis | Not covered by (6) cuticle. Made up of a layer of closely-packed thin-walled cells. Protects the inner tissues from injury and (7) infections. Some cells have (8) root hairs which provide a large (9) surface area for the absorption of water and minerals. |
| Cortex | Made up of layers of loosely-packed thin-walled cells. Stores food in the form of (10) starch. Allows the passage of water and minerals across the root. |
| Vascular bundle | Xylem transports (11) water and (12) minerals. Phloem transports (13) organic nutrients. |
| Root cap | Covers and protects the root tip. Constantly worn away and replaced by new cells produced in the root tip. |

### B. Process of absorption of water by roots (Book 1B, p. 10-20)

1. Water is lost from the leaves continuously by (14) transpiration. This creates transpiration pull.
2. Water is drawn up the (15) xylem vessels from the roots to the leaves by the transpiration pull.
3. Water in the cortex cells near the xylem vessels of the roots enters the xylem vessels. This decreases the water potential of them. A (16) water potential gradient is set up across the whole cortex.
4. In the cortex, water travels from cell to cell through three routes:

   a. Water moves along a water potential gradient through the (17) cytoplasm of cells by osmosis.

   b. Water moves along the same water potential gradient through the (18) vacuoles of cells by osmosis.

   c. Water travels from one cell to another freely through the (19) cell wall.

5. As water is drawn away from the root hair cells to the neighbouring cortical cells, the water potential of the root hair cells becomes lower. As the water potential of the soil water is (20) higher (lower / higher) than that of the root hair cells, water in the soil enters the root hair cells by (21) osmosis.

### C. Process of absorption of minerals by roots (Book 1B, p. 10-21)

- The concentrations of minerals in the soil are usually lower than that in the root hair cells. Minerals are mainly absorbed against the concentration gradient by (22) active transport.
- Active absorption of minerals by root hair cells (23) lowers (lowers / raises) the water potential of the cells. This helps the absorption of water into the cells by (24) osmosis.
- On rare occasions, when the concentrations of minerals in the soil are higher than that in the root hair cells, minerals enter the root hair cells down the concentration gradient by (25) diffusion.

### D. Adaptive features of roots for absorption of water and minerals (Book 1B, p. 10-22)

| Feature | Adaptation |
| --- | --- |
| Highly branched and have numerous (26) root hairs | Provide a large (27) surface area for the absorption of water and minerals. |
| Long and fine root hairs | Allows root hairs to grow between soil particles to absorb water and minerals. |
| Epidermis not covered by (28) cuticle and made up of one layer of thin-walled cells only | Allows water and minerals to pass through the epidermis into the root easily. |
| Many (29) mitochondria in root hair cells | Ensures enough energy is supplied to absorb minerals from the soil by active transport. |

## 10.3 Transport in plants (Book 1B, p. 10-24)

### A. Types of vascular tissues (Book 1B, p. 10-24)

#### (1) Xylem

**Function**

- Transports water and minerals.
- Provides support for plants.

**Adaptive feature**

- Mainly consists of (2) xylem vessels (木質導管) which are hollow tubes made up of (3) dead (living / dead) cells.
- Hollow tube (with no cell contents and no end walls between cells) allows the passage of water with little resistance.
- Cell walls are thick and (4) lignified (木質化的), which prevents vessels from collapsing under the great tension of transpiration pull.

#### (5) Phloem

**Function**

- Transports organic nutrients (mainly sugars).

**Adaptive feature**

- Mainly consists of (6) sieve tubes (篩管) and companion cells (伴細胞). Sieve tubes are elongated (7) living (living / dead) cells joined end to end.
- Sieve tube contains little cytoplasm and no (8) nucleus, which allows organic nutrients to move along with little resistance.
- Sieve plate (篩板) has (9) pores, which allows organic nutrients to pass through.
- Companion cell has dense cytoplasm and many organelles (including nucleus), which supports metabolism of the sieve tube.

### B. Distribution of vascular tissues (Book 1B, p. 10-26)

- In flowering plants, vascular tissues are arranged in long strands called (10) vascular bundles.
- Distributions of vascular bundles in the root, stem and leaf in a young dicotyledonous plant:

#### Leaf

- Diagram labels: (11) xylem, (12) phloem
- Vascular bundles are found in the large central (13) midrib and the network of small veins.
- Xylem lies on (14) top of phloem.

#### Stem

- Diagram labels: (15) xylem, (16) phloem
- Vascular bundles are arranged in a ring at the (17) periphery.
- (18) Xylem (Xylem / Phloem) is located in the inner region and (19) phloem (xylem / phloem) in the outer region.

#### Root

- Diagram labels: (20) xylem, (21) phloem
- Vascular bundles are located at the (22) centre.
- Phloem is found between the arms of xylem.

### C. Process of transport of water and minerals (Book 1B, p. 10-28)

1. Water is lost from leaves through (23) transpiration. Transpiration pull is created.
2. Water (with dissolved minerals) is drawn up the (24) xylem vessels by transpiration pull as a continuous stream.
3. Water in the soil is absorbed into the root by (25) osmosis. Dissolved minerals are absorbed by (26) active transport.

### D. Translocation of organic nutrients in plants (Book 1B, p. 10-30)

- Organic nutrients (carbohydrates) are synthesized in the leaves by photosynthesis and then transported by (27) phloem to other parts of the plant where they are used or stored.
- The transport of organic nutrients in plants is called (28) translocation (輸導). It is an (29) active process (i.e. requires energy).

## 10.4 Support in plants (Book 1B, p. 10-35)

- Importance of support (支持) to terrestrial plants:

  - To hold the leaves in the best position to receive the maximum amount of (1) sunlight for photosynthesis.
  - To lift up the flowers to facilitate (2) pollination.
  - To lift up the fruits and seeds to facilitate their (3) dispersal.

- Support in plants is mainly provided by the (4) turgidity of thin-walled cells and the (5) rigidity of thick-walled cells.

### A. Turgidity of thin-walled cells (Book 1B, p. 10-35)

Turgidity of thin-walled cells provides support to leaves, herbaceous stems and other non-woody plant parts of a plant.

| Adequate water supply | Inadequate water supply |
| --- | --- |
| Thin-walled cells in the stems and leaves gain water by osmosis. They become (6) turgid and press against each other. This gives support to the plants. The plant stands upright. | Thin-walled cells in the stems and leaves lose water. They become (7) flaccid and can no longer support the plant. The plant wilts. |

### B. Rigidity of thick-walled cells (Book 1B, p. 10-36)

- (8) Xylem vessels and the tissue outside each vascular bundle in young dicot stems consist of thick-walled cells. Their cell walls contain (9) lignin, which makes the cells hard and rigid. The rigidity of these cells supports the plant.
- When woody plant grows, more xylem is formed in woody stems and more lignin is deposited in the cell walls of the older xylem. The older xylem finally becomes (10) wood, which provides great support to the plants.
